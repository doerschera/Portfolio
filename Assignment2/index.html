Hello

I'm a print and web designer based in Austin, TX. My design aesthetic favors simplicity and ease of use in conjunction with bright, clean colors and linear forms. I have always been drawn to narrative and try to use design as a vehicle for storytelling, wether that story is text-based or client-based. I received a Bachelor of Fine Arts from the Rhode Island School of design and also spent a year in the Master of Architecture program at the University of Michigan. Outside of design work, I am a published poet and like to relax with knitting and podcasts.

One Strange Bird

One Strange Bird is an artist-run store on the West side of Chicago. The store specializes in unique and local hand-made goods in addition to providing community art classes for kids and adults. I began working with the store at its conception and helped to create initial branding through posters, postcards, and brochures. The store also hosted several opening and events, including the “30/30” show, which celebrated young female artists and raised money for a local women’s shelter. This set of postcard and poster was challenging as it necessitated organizing many disparate images into one cohesive package.

After Remix

“What Comes After Remix?” is an essay by Lev Manovich that explores the cultural implications of appropriation and sampling across multiple disciplines. In this project the text has been set in a twenty-page booklet, accompanied by a series of found images generated by cutting a five-by-five inch square through a magazine. In addition to the images and color overlays, the type was also used a formal device, highlighting to the idea of playfulness inherent in remixing.

Maier Catalogue

This booklet combines the story and work of Vivian Maier, whose boxes of undeveloped negatives were only recently discovered and processed. The hidden trove revealed an expansive body of images, primarily made using black and white film. The photos favor a documentary street style, often depicting people and urban landscapes. To complement the images, these layouts focused on clean, simple arrangements that highlight the narrative relationships between images.

RISD Card

TThese cards were developed for the Rhode Island School of Design's admissions office. The goal was create a guest card that was compatible with a new online registration system that collects visitor’s information prior to their arrival on campus. The card responds to several needs of the office and guests: First, it facilitates an interaction between the visitor and office staff as each visitor is greeted with a personalized card, viewbook, and map. Second, the card allows staff to keep track of information, allowing guests to update incorrect information, and providing staff with a quick visual of the number of attending guests at an event. Finally, the card provides a small glimpse into the lifestyle and atmosphere of RISD and encourages guests to personalize it with their own notes and doodles. The cards are first printed flat to accommodate the over-printing of guest information via a normal desk printer and are then folded when they are inserted into each viewbook. A perforation at the fold allows the information side of the card to be detached if a correction is need.

Architectural Illustration

Architectural representations range from scenographic to technical. This first project depicts a proposal for a hotel and sensory deprivation spa sited in an up and coming Toronto neighborhood. The goal of these drawings is to depict an accurate representation of both space and activity. A 90-90 axonometric projection is used as a tool of obfuscation, mimicking the possible disorienting effects of emerging from a sensory deprivation experience. Similarly, the extreme act of sensory deprivation is reflected in the stark colors and forms. The second illustration is one of three panels that depicts a whimsical scene, which was generated from random subjects. These subjects were suburbia, equestrian horse jumping, and the styling of TeamLab's Flower and Corpse Glitch Set, which features a digital wireframe cloud. This project was created with Lani Barry and Lindsey Karasik.

Poster

“poured from, pinned to”   was a three-person show that explored themes of domesticity, uitlity, and the body through sculpture and installation based work. The show was a part of a rotating series put on by undergraduate thesis students in RISD’s Sculpture Department. This series of posters sought to transform the familiar into the uncanny by divorcing the objects from their normal context. The title “poured from, pinned to” reflects the process and verbs used to created many of the works, which included mutated tableware, cast wax socks, leaning rubber forms, and milk frozen in the shape of tub spouts

Twilit Ruminations, Faltering Obsession

This piece, as well as the next, investigate the psychology of domestic spaces. We often think of domestic spaces as inherently safe, a haven from the anxiety of the outside world. Instead, I sought to reveal the latent discomfort of interior spaces and the possibility of a reciprocal relationship between psychology and space. The white wall has three peep holes, two of which are covered by the sheet and another blocked by the frosted acrylic-- the facade is both perorated and inaccessible. The second side of the piece is a narrow corridor painted with dark blue colors. The two tub spouts are made of frozen milk, both a symbol of night time comfort and a strange material of its own right. As the exhibition continues, the milk spouts begin to melt, allowing the milk to pool and run beneath the white barrier.

As if I were as Easy to Turn as a Knob

This piece combines sickly sweet color with looming threat. The arrangement of the cabinet, lamp, and rope mimics the form a box trap. Both the cabinet and floor below are covered in wallpaper, hinting camouflage as well as collusion. These objects are personified, begging the question of what happens in an empty home.
